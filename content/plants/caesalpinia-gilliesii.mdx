<span className="first">Y</span>es — Mexican Bird of Paradise is toxic to cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/bird-paradise) lists *Caesalpinia gilliesii* — also catalogued under [Poinciana](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/poinciana) — as toxic to cats, dogs, and horses. The toxic principle is described as GI irritants; clinical signs include intense oral burning, excessive drooling, vomiting, diarrhea, difficulty swallowing, and rarely incoordination. Deaths in rabbits have been reported, though severe cases in cats are uncommon.

Symptoms typically begin within minutes (oral burn, drooling) and progress to vomiting and diarrhea over a few hours. Most cats recover within 24 to 48 hours with supportive care. Seed-pod ingestions are the higher-risk exposures and the ones that can progress to incoordination.

### Three plants share the name "Bird of Paradise"

This is the source of most confusion in plant safety guides. In the US, three distinct plants are sold as some variant of "Bird of Paradise":

1. **Mexican / Yellow Bird of Paradise** — *Caesalpinia gilliesii*, a desert shrub with yellow flowers and long red stamens. The page you are reading.
2. **Pride of Barbados / Red Bird of Paradise** — *Caesalpinia pulcherrima*, a related tropical shrub. Also ASPCA toxic.
3. **Houseplant Bird of Paradise / Crane Flower** — *Strelitzia reginae*, the banana-leaved indoor floor plant. Also toxic but milder — see our [Strelitzia reginae](/plants/strelitzia-reginae) page.

All three are on the ASPCA toxic list. The Caesalpinia species have the stronger oral-burn / GI syndrome; Strelitzia produces a milder nausea and drowsiness picture, mostly from fruit and seeds.

### Where cats meet this plant

*Caesalpinia gilliesii* is a workhorse xeriscape shrub in the US Southwest, parts of Mexico, and similar warm-dry climates. It is a fast-growing, drought-tolerant flowering shrub with bright yellow flowers and dramatic red stamens that bloom from late spring through summer. Outdoor cats and patio-roaming indoor cats encounter it in foundation plantings and along property lines.

The seed pods — long, hairy, flat legumes — are the higher-risk parts. They split open as they dry, and cats can carry them in or chew them where they fall.

### Safer warm-garden alternatives

For flowering shrubs in the same xeriscape role without the GI irritants, [roses](/plants/rose) are the obvious replacement. Indoors, [African violets](/plants/african-violet) and [orchids](/plants/orchid) bring colour without the risk. See our [oleander](/plants/oleander) and [azalea](/plants/azalea) pages for the other landscape flowering shrubs cats should not be near.

### If exposure has happened

Call your vet or [ASPCA Animal Poison Control](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control) at (888) 426-4435. Rinse the cat's mouth with cool water if it tolerates it. Treatment is supportive — anti-nausea drugs, IV fluids for dehydration, and monitoring for the rare incoordination cases. Most cats recover fully within 24 to 48 hours.
