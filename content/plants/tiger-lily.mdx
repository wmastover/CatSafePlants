Yes — tiger lily (*Lilium tigrinum*, also classified as *Lilium lancifolium*) is toxic to cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/aspca-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/tiger-lily) states: *"Vomiting, inappetence, lethargy, kidney failure, and death is possible. Cats are only species known to be affected."*

## Why tiger lilies are deadly

Tiger lily belongs to the genus *Lilium* — the true lilies — in the family Liliaceae. Every true lily contains a toxin that is specifically and severely **nephrotoxic** to cats: it attacks the kidneys. Following ingestion, cats can develop **acute renal failure within 24 to 72 hours**, and without prompt veterinary treatment **mortality approaches 100 percent**. Dogs and other animals do not suffer the same kidney injury; cats are uniquely vulnerable.

There is no established safe dose. Documented cases of fatal poisoning have involved as little as a few leaves, a small amount of pollen, or a sip of vase water.

### Every part — and the water — is dangerous

The toxin is present throughout the plant: petals, leaves, stem, pollen, and the water a cut lily has stood in.

- **Pollen** — a cat that brushes past the flower picks up the bright orange pollen on its coat, then swallows it while grooming. No chewing is required.
- **Vase water** — water from a lily arrangement concentrates the toxin; a cat drinking from the vase can be poisoned without touching the flower.
- **Fallen petals and leaves** — dropped plant material on the floor or counter is within easy reach of a curious cat.

### What to watch for

Early signs are vomiting, drooling, loss of appetite (inappetence), and lethargy, often within a few hours. As the kidneys are injured, you may see changes in thirst and urination, progressing to no urine output — a sign the kidneys are shutting down. **Do not wait for symptoms to appear or worsen before seeking help.**

### What to do right now

1. Call **ASPCA Animal Poison Control at (888) 426-4435** or your veterinarian immediately.
2. Do **not** induce vomiting unless a professional directs you to.
3. If there is visible pollen on your cat's fur, wipe and wash it off so it cannot be groomed and swallowed.
4. Bring the plant (or a photo) to the vet to confirm the species.

Follow our **[full emergency guide for a cat that ate a lily](/emergency/cat-ate-a-lily/)** for what happens at the clinic and why time matters.

### Safe flowering alternatives

If you love flowers but live with cats, avoid all true lilies and daylilies. Safe, ASPCA-listed alternatives include [orchids](/plants/orchid/), [roses](/plants/rose/), and [African violets](/plants/african-violet/).
