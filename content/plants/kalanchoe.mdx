<span className="first">Y</span>es — kalanchoe is toxic to cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/kalanchoe) lists it as toxic, and unlike most flowering succulents its toxin is genuinely worth respecting: bufadienolide cardiac glycosides, compounds that act on the heart. The everyday reality is milder — vomiting, drooling, and diarrhoea — but large ingestions, especially of the flowers, can disturb heart rhythm.

That two-tier risk is the key thing to understand. A cat that nibbles a leaf will most likely just be sick. A cat that works through a flower head has eaten the most concentrated part and deserves a prompt call to the vet, watching for weakness, collapse, or an irregular pulse.

### A toxic-succulent family

Kalanchoe sits beside the [jade plant](/plants/jade-plant) in the Crassulaceae, and like [aloe vera](/plants/aloe-vera) it is one of the "harmless-looking succulent" traps. The flowering varieties — florist kalanchoe, mother of millions — are the ones most often brought indoors for colour.

### Safe swaps

For long-lasting flowers without the heart risk, [Christmas cactus](/plants/christmas-cactus), [African violet](/plants/african-violet), and [bromeliad](/plants/bromeliad) are all ASPCA non-toxic and just as cheerful.
