Do not wait for symptoms.** Daylilies are among the deadliest plants for cats — just as dangerous as true lilies. Yes — daylily (*Hemerocallis graminea*, and *Hemerocallis* species generally) is toxic to cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/aspca-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/orange-day-lily) states: *"Vomiting, inappetence, lethargy, kidney failure, and death is possible. Cats are only species known to be affected."*

## The dangerous "not a real lily" myth

Here is the correction that saves cats' lives: **daylilies ARE NOT true lilies (*Lilium*) botanically, but they ARE equally deadly to cats.**

People reason that because a daylily is not a "real" lily, it must be harmless. That reasoning is wrong and it kills cats. *Hemerocallis* contains the same kind of kidney toxin as true lilies, and a cat that ingests it can die of acute renal failure within days.

The ASPCA classifies *Hemerocallis* under the family **Liliaceae** for historical reasons. Modern botanists place daylilies in a separate family, **Asphodelaceae** — they are not in the genus *Lilium* at all. None of that taxonomy matters at the food bowl: the verdict is the same. Deadly.

## Why daylilies are deadly

The toxin is specifically **nephrotoxic** to cats — it attacks the kidneys. After ingestion, cats can develop **acute renal failure within 24 to 72 hours**, and without prompt veterinary treatment **mortality approaches 100 percent**. Dogs and other species do not suffer the same injury; cats are uniquely vulnerable.

There is no established safe dose. Fatal poisonings have followed a few bites of leaf or flower, a small amount of pollen, or a sip of vase water.

### Every part — and the water — is dangerous

- **Pollen** — picked up on the coat and swallowed during grooming.
- **Vase water** — water from a daylily arrangement concentrates the toxin.
- **Leaves and flowers** — the strap-like leaves are within reach of an indoor cat; the blooms attract curiosity.

### What to do right now

1. Call **ASPCA Animal Poison Control at (888) 426-4435** or your veterinarian immediately.
2. Do **not** induce vomiting unless directed by a professional.
3. Wash any visible pollen from your cat's fur.
4. Bring the plant or a photo to confirm the species.

See our **[full emergency guide for a cat that ate a lily](/emergency/cat-ate-a-lily/)** for what happens at the clinic.

### Safe alternatives

Avoid all lilies — *Lilium* and *Hemerocallis* alike. Safe, ASPCA-listed flowers include [orchids](/plants/orchid/), [roses](/plants/rose/), and [gerbera daisies](/plants/gerbera-daisy/). For an overview of which "lilies" are and are not dangerous, see our [general lily guide](/plants/lily/) and the deadly [tiger lily page](/plants/tiger-lily/).
