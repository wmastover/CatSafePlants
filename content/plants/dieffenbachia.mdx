<span className="first">Y</span>es — dieffenbachia is toxic to cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/animal-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/dieffenbachia) lists the dumb cane as toxic, and it is one of the harsher members of its family: alongside the usual insoluble calcium oxalate crystals it carries a proteolytic enzyme that amplifies the reaction. Chewing causes severe oral pain and pronounced swelling of the mouth, lips, and tongue, with heavy drooling and vomiting.

The common name "dumb cane" comes from that swelling, which can temporarily impair speech in people — and in cats produces frantic pawing at the face and a mouth too sore to use. Most cases stay confined to the mouth and gut, but the marked swelling makes airway compromise a more real (if still rare) concern than with milder aroids.

### The arum family, at its sharpest

Dieffenbachia shares its toxin with [pothos](/plants/pothos), [aglaonema](/plants/aglaonema), and [anthurium](/plants/anthurium), but the added enzyme makes it the one to treat most seriously of the group. Watch breathing closely after any ingestion.

### Safe swaps

For the same large, leafy presence, [calathea](/plants/calathea) brings patterned drama, a [cast-iron plant](/plants/cast-iron-plant) is tough and glossy, and a [parlor palm](/plants/parlor-palm) fills a floor corner softly — all ASPCA non-toxic.
