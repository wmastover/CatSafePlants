<span className="first">Y</span>es — the magnolia is safe for cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/aspca-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/magnolia-bush) lists the magnolia bush as *"Non-Toxic to Dogs, Non-Toxic to Cats, Non-Toxic to Horses."* The plant, its leaves, and its flowers pose no poisoning risk.

Magnolia (*Magnolia stellata*, the star magnolia) is a deciduous shrub or small tree in the family Magnoliaceae, prized for its early, star-shaped white blooms. Whether grown as a patio specimen, a border shrub, or a small garden tree, a cat that bats at a fallen petal or chews a leaf is in no danger.

## ASPCA Data

According to the ASPCA, the magnolia bush is listed under plants non-toxic to cats:

> Non-Toxic to Dogs, Non-Toxic to Cats, Non-Toxic to Horses.

There are no toxic principles. The star magnolia is completely safe around cats.

## A note on which magnolia

This is worth being precise about. The ASPCA entry is specifically for the **magnolia bush — *Magnolia stellata*, the star magnolia.** That is the species we can state with confidence is verified non-toxic.

The genus *Magnolia* includes many other plants — the saucer magnolia (*Magnolia* × *soulangeana*), the evergreen southern magnolia (*Magnolia grandiflora*), and dozens more. These share the same benign family chemistry and are **highly likely to be safe**, but they have **not been individually ASPCA-verified.** If you want a magnolia you can point to a published ASPCA listing for, the star magnolia is it; for other species the safe assumption is reasonable but rests on family chemistry rather than a species-specific entry.

## Living with a magnolia around cats

Because the plant is non-toxic, there is no safety reason to fence it off. A few practical notes:

- **Falling petals and leaves.** Magnolias shed thick petals and leathery leaves. The litter is harmless if nibbled, though a cat may happily chase a falling bloom.
- **Bulk matters, not toxicity.** Eating a large quantity of any plant matter can cause mild, self-limiting stomach upset — that is volume, not poison.

## Growing a magnolia

Magnolias want full sun to part shade, rich and slightly acidic free-draining soil that stays moist, and shelter from strong wind that can tear the large blooms. Early-flowering star magnolias appreciate protection from harsh morning sun after a frost, which can brown the petals.

## The bottom line

The star magnolia is a safe, ASPCA-verified plant for homes and gardens with cats, and its relatives are highly likely safe on the same family chemistry. For other safe, fragrant garden shrubs, see [camellia](/plants/camellia/) and [crepe myrtle](/plants/crepe-myrtle/). Note that [gardenia](/plants/gardenia/) — a fragrant shrub often grown alongside magnolias — is *toxic* to cats, so do not confuse the two.
