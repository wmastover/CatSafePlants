<span className="first">Y</span>es — cherry trees are toxic to cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/aspca-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/cherry) lists *Prunus spp.* as **Toxic to Dogs, Toxic to Cats, Toxic to Horses**. The stems, leaves, and pits contain cyanogenic glycosides that release cyanide when chewed or digested. The ASPCA notes the Prunus group also includes plum, peach, and [apricot](/plants/apricot/) — all toxic by the same mechanism.

Cherry (*Prunus spp.*, family **Rosaceae**) is a common garden and orchard tree. The cyanogenic glycosides are concentrated in the **stems, leaves, and pits** — not the flesh of the fruit. The danger for an outdoor cat is chewing on fallen cherries (which exposes the pits), nibbling low-hanging leaves, or playing with pruned branches.

## ASPCA Data

According to the ASPCA, cherry is listed under plants toxic to cats:

> Toxic to Dogs, Toxic to Cats, Toxic to Horses.

The toxic parts are the stems, leaves, and pits, which contain **cyanogenic glycosides** — compounds that release cyanide when the plant tissue is damaged. The ASPCA notes the Prunus group also includes plum, peach, and apricot.

## Why the stems, leaves, and pits are dangerous

Cyanogenic glycosides are a chemical defence mechanism. When a cat chews the stems, leaves, or pits, the plant cells rupture and an enzyme releases **cyanide** from the glycoside. Cyanide blocks cellular respiration — it prevents cells from using oxygen, effectively suffocating them at the cellular level.

The fruit flesh is the exception: it contains very low levels of cyanogenic glycosides and is generally considered safe. The ASPCA listing, however, covers the whole plant, so the safe approach is to treat the entire tree as off-limits.

## The Prunus family

Cherry belongs to the genus *Prunus*, which the ASPCA notes also includes **plum, peach, and [apricot](/plants/apricot/)** — all toxic to cats by the same cyanogenic glycoside mechanism. [Apple](/plants/apple/) is a different genus (*Malus*) but the same **Rosaceae** family and the same cyanogenic toxin. If you are cautious about one, the same caution applies to the others — the family is consistent.

## Risk for outdoor cats

An outdoor cat with access to a cherry tree faces two main risks:

- **Fallen cherries.** Windfall fruit attracts cats to play with and chew. The pits inside are the toxic part — a cat that bites into a windfall cherry ingests them directly.
- **Wilted leaves and pruned branches.** Wilted cherry leaves are particularly dangerous because the wilting process concentrates the cyanogenic glycosides. Remove pruned branches and fallen leaves promptly.

## What to do if your cat eats cherry stems, leaves, or pits

Cyanide toxicity is a medical emergency:

1. **Call your vet or ASPCA Animal Poison Control at (888) 426-4435 immediately.** Cyanide acts fast — do not wait for symptoms.
2. Remove any plant material from your cat's mouth if you can do so safely.
3. Tell the vet exactly what part of the cherry tree was eaten and how much.

## Safe garden alternatives

If you want a cat-safe fruiting or flowering plant, [strawberry](/plants/strawberry/), [sunflower](/plants/sunflower/), and [calendula](/plants/calendula/) are all ASPCA-listed non-toxic to cats — and they grow well in the same garden conditions as a cherry tree.

## The bottom line

Cherry trees are toxic to cats — the stems, leaves, and pits contain cyanogenic glycosides that release cyanide. The Prunus group (cherry, plum, peach, [apricot](/plants/apricot/)) and [apple](/plants/apple/) all share the same mechanism. The fruit flesh is generally safe, but with a cat in the garden, the safe approach is to keep the whole tree off-limits and clear windfall cherries promptly.
