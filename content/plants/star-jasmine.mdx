<span className="first">Y</span>es — star jasmine is safe for cats. The [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/aspca-poison-control/toxic-and-non-toxic-plants/star-jasmine) lists *Trachelospermum jasminoides* as *"Non-Toxic to Dogs, Non-Toxic to Cats, Non-Toxic to Horses."* The flowers, leaves, and stems pose no poisoning risk.

But the word **"jasmine" is one of the most dangerous common names in the plant world** — it is attached to at least six different plants, and three of them are toxic to cats. So before you relax, confirm which one you actually have.

## "Jasmine" disambiguation — six plants, one name

| Common name | Botanical name | For cats |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **True Jasmine** | *Jasminum* spp. | **Safe** — see [jasmine](/plants/jasmine/) |
| **Star Jasmine** | *Trachelospermum jasminoides* | **Safe** — this page |
| **Madagascar Jasmine** | *Stephanotis floribunda* | **Safe** — see [Madagascar jasmine](/plants/madagascar-jasmine/) |
| **Cape Jasmine** | *Gardenia jasminoides* | **Toxic** — see [gardenia](/plants/gardenia/) |
| **Carolina Jessamine** | *Gelsemium sempervirens* | **Toxic** |
| **Night-Blooming Jasmine** | *Cestrum nocturnum* | **Toxic** |

The takeaway is simple: **the common name "jasmine" cannot tell you whether a plant is safe.** Only the Latin name can. This page is about **star jasmine, *Trachelospermum jasminoides***, which is non-toxic.

## ASPCA Data

According to the ASPCA, star jasmine is listed under plants non-toxic to cats:

> Non-Toxic to Dogs, Non-Toxic to Cats, Non-Toxic to Horses.

There are no toxic principles. Star jasmine is completely safe around cats.

## Not a true jasmine

Despite the name, star jasmine is **not a *Jasminum*.** It belongs to the genus *Trachelospermum* in the family **Apocynaceae** — the same family as oleander and other notoriously toxic plants. That family relationship is worth noting precisely *because* it shows why you cannot reason about toxicity from family alone: *Trachelospermum* is ASPCA-verified non-toxic even though some of its relatives are dangerous.

Star jasmine does share the family's milky sap. The plant is non-toxic, but the sap may cause minor, transient skin or mouth irritation if a cat chews a lot of stem — an irritant nuisance, not a poisoning.

## Growing star jasmine around cats

Star jasmine is an evergreen twining vine that flowers most freely in full sun, though it tolerates part shade. Give it a trellis, wire, or obelisk to climb, keep the soil evenly moist in the growing season with free draining soil, and prune after flowering. Because it is non-toxic, there is no safety reason to keep a cat away — only to protect the vine from chewing.

## The bottom line

Star jasmine (*Trachelospermum jasminoides*) is safe for cats — but its name is shared by toxic plants, so always confirm the Latin name. The safe "jasmines" are this one, [true jasmine](/plants/jasmine/), and [Madagascar jasmine](/plants/madagascar-jasmine/); the toxic ones include [gardenia](/plants/gardenia/) (Cape jasmine), Carolina jessamine, and night-blooming jasmine.
